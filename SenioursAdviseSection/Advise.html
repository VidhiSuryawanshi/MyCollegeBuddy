🎓 Seniors’ Advice Section

💡 Mistakes Most Students Make (But You Can Avoid)

  • Waiting till the last semester to start coding or preparing for placements. Start early — even in 2nd year! Trust me, even solving one problem daily or building small projects consistently will compound over time.
  • Just mugging up theory instead of doing hands-on practice. Projects, debugging, errors — that's how real learning happens. Interviews test logic and problem-solving, not textbook memory.
  • Being too focused on CGPA. Yes, it’s important — but recruiters also care about your practical skills, communication, and ability to solve real problems. Balance both!
  • Making resumes look flashy but adding nothing genuine. If you’ve built a basic calculator but write “engineered dynamic computation system” — it’s obvious. Be honest and explain your work well.
  • Thinking “DSA is only for big companies”. Even service-based companies ask logic-based questions. DSA isn’t just for Amazon or Google — it's a mindset booster.
  • Stressing too much. Placement season is tough, yes. But comparison is poison. Some get in early, some late — both paths are okay. Keep learning and believe in your timing.
  • Not networking. Just one message to a senior can change your prep game. Don’t hesitate to reach out, ask for tips, and stay connected.
  • Ignoring communication skills. Even if you're a coding champ, if you can't explain your thought process clearly, you may lose the edge.
  • Copy-pasting projects from GitHub. You’ll be stuck in interviews. Build small, simple things, but build them yourself — that’s what counts.
  • Neglecting mental health. Binge-coding, skipping sleep, and constant anxiety won’t help. Take breaks. Your mind is your best tool — keep it healthy.

🗣️ Advice from Seniors Who’ve Been There

Ritika Sharma (Final Year, CSE)

“I used to think I wasn’t smart enough for product-based companies. But I started solving 1 DSA problem daily, and it built my confidence. You don’t need 10 projects, you need 2 that you understand well. Also, take your resume seriously — it’s your story on paper. And please, practice explaining your projects aloud before interviews.”

Aditya Mehta (3rd Year, CSE)

“If you’re an introvert, don’t worry — you don’t need to talk a lot to stand out. Just start participating in coding contests, post on LinkedIn about your learning, and reach out to seniors for help. Most of us want to help juniors — just ask sincerely. Also, don’t fake things on your resume — if you haven’t done ML, don’t write ‘Python ML Expert’. Keep it real.”

“Also, keep a small journal of your progress. It helps you stay accountable and gives you motivation when you look back.”

Simran Kaur (Final Year, IT)

“Don’t ignore aptitude. I know it sounds boring, but if you’re aiming for companies like Infosys, Wipro, Capgemini — aptitude + communication can get you the offer even if you’re average in coding. Also, during interviews, stay calm. Even if you get stuck , don’t panic — just explain your thought process. They judge how you think, not how fast you type.”

“One more thing — take part in college hackathons and fests. They teach teamwork, time pressure, and give you memories too. Those moments will stay with you.”

Rahul Verma (Placed at Accenture)

“I was never a topper, but I practiced regularly. I watched free YouTube playlists and took notes. I didn’t just consume content — I implemented it. Consistency wins. Also, be kind to your batchmates — the tech world is small, and you’ll cross paths again.”

Priya Sinha (Placed at TCS)

“Soft skills helped me crack HR interviews. Read a little English daily, speak to friends in English, and watch interviews online. Prepare answers to questions like ‘Tell me about yourself’ and ‘Why should we hire you?’ Don’t sound like a robot — be real, smile, and speak from the heart.”
